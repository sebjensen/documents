Legal

Covering music on YouTube: The repercussions for covering and using music varies between different countries and songs. Typically, videos will be monetised by the copyright holders and/or claimant(s), and it is only in rare cases that a video will be blocked entirely. As this music product is not for profit/non-commercial, this should not affect us. We can also excercise the fair use exception of all applicable copyright laws due to the nature of this product and relation to the course.

28th April - 5th May 2021

These weeks we were recording our performances that we have been working on. I was the de facto "audio engineer" for the sessions, and brought in my microphone and laptop to record both drums and piano. We utilised session 3 on these days and carried on afterschool just playing our pieces that we had worked on and getting them into an audio format for editing.

After a bit of experimenting, I preferred the stereo pickup pattern for recording the drums and piano. As opposed to using the cardoid pickup pattern, which is more convential when utilising only a single microphone, I opted for stereo as it creates, in my opinion, more immesion. As the origin of sounds in terms of location are more emphasised with the stereo pickup pattern, it brings the listner into the recording area and in front of the drums and piano.

I recorded the raw audio directly into Audacity, and made use of Adobe's Audition software for audio refinements and amplification. A large part of this process was noise reduction - generating a background noise profile and then applying that to eliminate the background sounds that are picked up by microphones. Although not always applicable to recording instruments, I like to run most audio through the ENCN process - equalise, normalise, compress, normalise. Compression is more useful for speech, so podcasts and such, so, obviously, I adapted these processes.

These were straight forward sessions, but just so time consuming as we'd run through entire pieces multiple times to achieve the sound we wanted for each song.

29th May 2021

I've come across this incredibly useful open-source tool that will help us with editing "25 Years of Rock & Indie". It is called 'Spleeter' and is developed mainly by Deezer's in-house research team.

Spleeter is a source separation library, providing pre-trained models for various separation modes. Simply put, the library can be harnessed to split up the various layers that make up a song. It is designed to separate vocals, drums, bass, piano, and other instruments into separate audio files that can be mixed to your liking.

This is quite common for those that cover songs with their own vocals, as they can put the original song through Spleeter and reconstruct it without the original vocals layer. This results in a karaoke/instrumental track, and then you can put your own vocals over the top of the original song. This is useful because not many artists publicly share instrumental versions of their music with lyrics.

We'll be using this for a slightly different case, however. We'll be keeping the original vocal layer, but removing the piano and/or drum layers from certain songs, where we have recorded our own performances of them. Let's use 'Fake Tales of San Francisco', which will be performed by Owen on the drums. We'll keep all the other layers, but with Spleeter we'll be able to easily remove the drum layer and replace it with our own recording.

15th June 2021

Editing the individual videos is relatively straight-forward, and much less resource-intensive when compared to producing a full-length concert in one timeline. It consists of collecting and organising all the required assets, and for some songs this also includes video clips from the official music video (used in accordance to the fair use exception of copyright laws). We already have everything we need, such as all the video clips recorded in person and at home, our audio recordings, and the original song - and by ensuring these are intuitively organised and laid out can hasten the workflow considerably. Once all assets and this organisation has been accounted for, it is a matter of laying it out as desired in the NLE.

I am editing the videos with Adobe Premiere Pro, and will use Adobe Media Encoder to nicely queue up all the videos for export with the same settings once I finish all of them. The export will be in .mp4 format, H.264 encoding. This is widely supported by the vast majority of devices, but most importantly, is the standard for YouTube.

Evaluation

Objectively, the concert was relatively successful in various regards. I feel as though I have made a considerable contribution to the project, and we collaborated well to produce and present a finished product. There are a number of potential improvements that I have identified, along with numerous successes - both will be detailed as follows.

When we received the briefing in September 2020, Owen, Conrad, and I decided to work together on this project. We were already on good terms, making it easier to express our creativity and collaborate.

Project Selection

We opted for creating a virtual concert. This proved to be a good balance that made equal use of all of our skills. While a podcast would have appealed more to me, it would likely have reduced the contributions that Conrad and Owen would have been able to make as there are less performance opportunities. With a virtual concert, I would be able to take responsibility for video editing and production, with both Conrad and Owen being our prime performers.

Roles & Leadership

We delegated roles based on our individual strengths. I was in charge of technical elements, such as video editing and audio recording. I feel as though I was able to make a significant contribution to the project in this area, as I am much more confident with the technicalities as opposed to being the one performing.

Furthermore, I took on an extra level of leadership and served as the project manager for much of the process. I scheduled and set up meetings and official communication, as well as documenting it by recording videos and creating a minutes document for every meeting. I could then refer back to these documents and both formal and informal communication to guide the project and advance it into its next steps.

Conrad and Owen are both performers and practice their instruments much more than I do and play at a much better level. We decided that they would contribute the most with the performance elements.

This was a good decision, as it allowed us to focus on our separate areas and roles. However, it was a rigid setup and meant that we often did not help out with each other’s tasks or at least have much understanding about the progress being made in those areas. Next time, I think that we should either communicate more about the progress being made in each area or offer each other more opportunities to contribute to each section.

Communication & Teamwork

Over the course of the project, we communicated formally with meetings on Google Meet, and informally by messaging each other. I think there was a good balance between official and informal communication.

When we needed to address project progress and next steps, we set up meetings with minutes logged and a video recorded. This helped us to create an official record of what was discussed and in which meeting, along with the proposed next steps so we can see how effectively they were acted upon.

However, I feel as though we could have organised more meetings. When we did conduct meetings, they were very productive, but I don’t think there was enough formal communication, with us talking informally. This informal discussion is a lot harder to evaluate and document. I think we could improve this for next time by setting out regular intervals for meetings, regardless of the progress that has been made. For example, every 2-4 weeks would have been optimal.

Teamwork was fairly straight forward - we are good friends and have been for a while, so the collaboration was natural and we could rely on each other to contribute to the project. There's little else to add, as we never had any massive disagreements throughout the life of the project, and were unanimous in most decisions. This made the project a lot less stressful to work on, I believe.

Time Management & Release

I set out to account for time management by creating a project timeline in the form of a gantt chart. These charts are commonplace in many industries for laying down tasks and their progression. I was able to make a significant contribution here in doing so, and I was able to use this document to aid my leadership of the project and guide our group's next steps.

As the year unfolded, however, time management was a struggle due to the various additional commitments that each of us had outside of the project. COVID-19 and lockdown was a large contributor to these issues, as new protocols and restrictions within school prevented us from practicing during lunchtimes, which we could have done in previous years. Lockdown made it more difficult to practice our performances, and emphasised a more individual approach to project progress that impacted teamwork.

We were clearly too ambitious when beginning the project, and did not accurately account for the delays we'd encounter. That said, there's not much that could be done in terms of what was beyond our control. We, however, have learnt how to better mitigate these delays this year, and we have grown our personal skills in this area.

As for the release, I think our switch to uploading performances as individual videos in a series was beneficial. This positively impacted the project, as it allowed us to spend more time on each release, while keeping our opportunities open for the future. There are also potential links to other units within the music BTEC, which means we'll still be able to contribute to 'The Indie Bois' later on down the line. I'm pleased that we came to this decision, as I enjoyed getting to share our performances on the Internet as part of the course, and we'll be able to continue this.

We could have improved this switch by holding an official meeting or documenting a formal discussion. This change was only determined verbally in-person and in direct messages to one another, which makes this harder to evidence. A meeting would let us discuss all the implications of such a large change at once, rather than having many casual conversations strung over multiple days.
